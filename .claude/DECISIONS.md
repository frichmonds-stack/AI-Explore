## Strategy (2026-07-23, latest — the delivery rubric skeleton: Function / Form / Substance)

Second working session of the day, straight on from the posture/audience entry below. Moved from *why the site exists* into *the benchmark's engine*. Built from evidence, not invention: the owner wrote a real worksheet prompt, generated a real artefact (ChatGPT, "GPT Sol"), and we graded it against its own spec — the criteria fell out of that grading. All items **OWNER CALLs** unless flagged.

- **OWNER CALL — "score delivery only" survives re-examination, with an UPGRADED rationale.** The owner deliberately re-opened the banked neutrality premise ("can I be hesitant to be so cut and dry about whether pedagogy should be rewarded?") rather than inherit it. Worked through, it holds — but the *reason* changed. It was banked as a **neutrality/bias-dodge** argument; it is now a **predictability** argument, which is stronger and more useful: *"Bring your intent. The score tells you how faithfully and how well this tool will execute it — so you can trust what lands."* In the owner's words: **does the tool do what the teacher wants it to do, REGARDLESS of pedagogical hierarchy** — which is what lets the teacher *know what they are going to get*. A tool can score full marks on pedagogically ordinary material; that is the point, not a bug, because the score is honest about **fidelity**.
- **OWNER CALL — prompt quality is held constant (expert prompts); varying it is OUT OF SCOPE.** This closes the "render vs elevate" fork the agent raised: a tool's ability to *elevate a weak/lazy prompt* (real value, since real teachers don't write expert prompts) is **not measured**. You have to hold something constant to measure anything, and the prompt is it. The benchmark measures **render**, not elevate.
- **OWNER CALL — criteria must be SUBJECT-GENERAL.** The catch that killed "notation fidelity" as a standalone criterion: the owner spotted it as "very specific to STEM" — i.e. the positional-bias compass working exactly as designed, catching the owner's own home turf before it set the whole rubric. **Principle: any candidate criterion that only bites for one subject must generalise or fold in; subject-specific things live as *manifestations under* a general axis, never as their own axis.** Notation generalised into Form (its faces: stacked fractions in maths, staff notation in music, subscripts in chemistry, dialogue/line-breaks in English). **Consequence parked (not ruled):** a criterion's *weight* likely has to flex by artefact/subject — form is decisive for maths/music, near-weightless for a discussion prompt — even though the criteria themselves stay universal.
- **OWNER CALL — the "craft" / anti-slop axis is real and named for the quality, not the proxy.** The owner asked whether "obviously AI-generated / AI slop" should be scored. Resolved: **the fear isn't "it comes from GPT" — it's that "it's made from AI and it's terrible."** AI-made-and-great is fine; AI just makes terrible cheap and abundant. Therefore all detection-framed names were **rejected** (`Turing Test`, `Human Fidelity`, `AI Detection`) — naming the axis after detectability would score **disguise instead of quality**, and the goal was never to fool anyone. `Quality` rejected as too broad (all criteria are quality). Kept as **craft**: is the language/material *considered and purposeful* vs *hollow filler*. Matters little for symbolic artefacts (maths), enormously for text-heavy ones (comprehension passages, discussion questions, parent emails) where the words *are* the product. Closest candidate to the pedagogy line — held there by scoping it to *craft of the writing*, never *quality of the teaching*.
- **OWNER CALL — the skeleton is three buckets, two tiers: `Function / Form / Substance`.** Supersedes both the earlier "six flat delivery criteria" framing (BACKLOG) and the intermediate `Function/Content/Presentation`. The owner's own definitions: **Function** = does it produce an artefact/service that *performs as it's meant to*; **Form** = does it produce something that *works with our senses*; **Substance** = is the material *correct, accurate and aligned* (and, per the craft ruling, *any good*). Read as **works · reaches me · is what's inside any good**.

  | Bucket | Question | Sub-criteria |
  |---|---|---|
  | **Function** | Does it perform as meant? | works/runs · usable (room to work) · editable · technical integrity · interactivity |
  | **Form** | Does it reach the senses right? | layout · notation/form conventions · legibility & audibility · aesthetic |
  | **Substance** | Is the material correct — and any good? | accuracy · alignment to brief · completeness · sequence · **craft** (anti-slop) |

  Two-tier by design (the site's recurring dual-register/ACME pattern): a small legible top layer over a working layer. **This kills the "is it six?" question** — the top level stays small, the sub-level is as many as are *honest*, with no padding to a target number.
- **Sub-rulings inside the skeleton:**
  - **Vessel vs payload** is the Form/Substance line: **Form = is it well-rendered/perceivable; Substance = is the thing said or depicted actually good and on-brief.** A crisp, beautiful render of the *wrong or hollow* thing = Form ✓, Substance ✗.
  - **`Substance` over `Content`, and the triple-F was deliberately declined.** The owner floated `Function/Form/Fact` for memorability; rejected because **`Fact` would amputate craft** (and misreads for stories/discussion prompts, which aren't "facts"). Explicit call: don't let alliteration cost the most valuable, least-obvious axis found that day. `Fidelity` also rejected as a bucket name — overloaded (used generically across all buckets); `Fitness` rejected as bleeding into Function.
  - **Every bucket runs floor → ceiling**: *did what was asked* → *did it excellently*. Substance: correct → considered (craft). Form: renders → beautiful (aesthetic). Function: works → works well. **Aesthetic is therefore not its own criterion — it's the top of the Form scale.** Accepted cost: the aesthetic ceiling is the **most contestable, most subjective point in the whole rubric** (a great-looking Year 2 sheet ≠ a great-looking Year 11 one), included knowingly because polish is real value.
  - **No double-counting.** If something is unusable *because* Form or Substance failed, it is billed **once, upstream, where the fault is** — Function is not a second bill. Function scores only the operational residue nothing else explains (runs, editable, prints, interactive). Prompted by the owner's observation that "if either content or presentation fail enough, it becomes unusable" — i.e. usability is largely **emergent** from the other two, not an independent peer.
  - **Why Function survives as its own bucket** despite that emergence: the benchmark scores **tools**, not just documents. For interactive/editable outputs (quiz apps, editable docs, slide exports) "does it actually run / can I adapt it" is independent of looks — a slick quiz with broken answer-checking, a beautiful doc delivered as an uneditable flat image.

**Evidence note (worth keeping):** the test artefact was **better than the owner expected** — correct maths in all 3 worked models, all 12 questions correctly categorised into the three denominator types, properly *stacked* fraction notation, working lines + answer lines. The owner's prior experience ("outputs are just text on a page… ugly and actually unusable") did **not** reproduce, plausibly because the prompt was far more exact and the model more capable. **Implication:** with prompt quality held constant at "expert", accuracy and completeness trend toward table-stakes for strong models, so **the discriminating power of the benchmark will sit mostly in Form and Function** — the render/perform dimensions — not in content correctness.

**Still open:** the **0→4 scale descriptors** for each sub-criterion (the next and biggest stretch — where this stops being architecture and becomes a scoring tool); how criterion **weighting flexes by artefact/subject**; whether "the stance" is a fully named component; page structure & display (deferred).

## Strategy (2026-07-23, earlier — explanatory spine: posture, audience, refusals, win conditions)

First working session *authoring* the spine (structure/display deferred as a "later" problem — the owner's call was to answer the high-level convictions that direct all other effort first). All items below are **OWNER CALLs** unless flagged. These are the reference the rest of the site reconciles to.

- **The site is a first-person, opinionated voice — not a neutral aggregator.** It says "I think", takes sides, doesn't hide behind "some educators find". The site's view and the owner's view are one and the same, unapologetically. (Bonus: this half-answers the open "is the stance a *named component*" question — a first-person site can simply *say* the stance rather than formalise it as a component. Not fully closed; carry.)
- **Posture toward AI — "show, don't push".** The never-used-AI teacher is *both* "fine and complete" *and* "missing leverage" — both true at once; the difference was never about the teacher but about the response. The owner doesn't tell people what to do: where there is value, people implement in their own time/journey; persuasion doesn't move the tech-hesitant, readiness does. The job is to *show and tell* what's possible. AI is one option among many.
- **Soft on tools, firm on craft.** The craft is *irreducible, like any skill* — a real thing to be good at, with better/worse ways. The AI tool is optional (just an instrument); good teaching is not optional (the actual work). "Like any skill" also carries the earlier floor-vs-art reconciliation: fundamentals are irrefutable, expression is personal art (a musician has both). The stance connects the two temperatures: trust the teacher to pick tools, hold them to the craft.
- **Audience: K–12 educators, broadly.** The owner's high-school-STEM background is **not** a scoping device — it does not narrow who benefits.
- **Positional bias REVISED — internal compass, not public disclosure.** Supersedes the 2026-07-22 framing (part 1 of the bias declaration below) that cast it as a reader-facing "calibrate for my angle" disclosure. New ruling: the owner's high-school-STEM strength (and corresponding deficit/inexperience in primary, humanities, early years) is a **build-time compass between owner and agent** — it tells us where content needs shoring up, outside expertise, or extra care before it's trusted. Default (a) internal only; (b) surfaces publicly *only where it adds value to a reader* (e.g. flagging a primary example is off the owner's home turf), never as ritual disclaimer. The **epistemic** bias (science-of-learning floor) stays public and defended — unchanged.
- **What the site refuses to be — eight "not that"s** (internal conviction now; the trust-building ones 3/4/5/9 are candidates to lift into public copy later, by the "adds value" rule): (1) not a catalogue of every tool — *I curate*; (2) not a news feed — *not chasing every release*; (3) not vendor-driven — *no paid placement*; (4) not telling you to adopt AI — *show, don't push*; (5) not a replacement for your judgement; (6) not an authority on your classroom — *I offer, you adapt*; (7) not a technical/coding resource — *no jargon walls*; (9) not treating kid safety as an afterthought — *safety is a gate*. (No 8: "not a prompt-engineering course" was **killed** — prompt technique is legitimate editorial in an AI space; the underlying "craft central, tools subordinate" value is already carried by 4/5.)
- **Win conditions — plural, teacher-relative, NO hierarchy; the site names no single win.** The goods (more learning, efficiency/productivity, reclaimed bandwidth inside & outside class, better choices, more options) don't rank against each other — what one teacher wants another ignores, depending on who they are and their stage. The benchmark **already encodes this**: it scores *delivery only*, and the teacher's priority lives in the *prompt they bring*, not in a site-imposed ranking. The agent's attempt to make "learning" the tiebreaker was **retracted** as over-prioritising.
- **The safety gate and the craft floor are the *frame*, NOT win conditions.** Owner correctly rejected folding either into the win-condition ranking. Neither is something you optimise toward or rank against efficiency. The **safety gate** is pass/fail and *lexically first* — an unsafe tool doesn't score low, it's disqualified; the six delivery criteria only apply to what cleared it. The **craft floor** lives on a *different layer* — embodied in which prompts get modelled as best-practice (the prompt layer), not in the score and not in the win conditions. This is why the floor felt "homeless" when discussed among win conditions: it isn't one. Shape: *a fixed frame (gate + floor) inside which the plural goods trade off freely, teacher by teacher.*
- **Win-condition tagging (CANDIDATE feature, not committed) — tag tools by capacity delivered, never by learning.** Idea: let teachers find tools by what they're for (saves time vs …). Feasible as a taxonomy facet and neutrality-safe *if* it describes what a tool is *aimed at*, not scores pedagogy. **The honest constraint:** only tag the *tool's* half of the chain — capacity it hands back (*saves time/effort*, *production* = makes materials, *widens options/ideas*, *frees in-class bandwidth*). Do **not** tag "makes you a better teacher" or "improves learning" — those are the *teacher's* half (craft converts capacity into learning), and crediting the tool for them is the boosterism the posture refuses. On "high-quality materials": *delivery* quality (accurate, on-brief, usable, editable) = what the six criteria score; *pedagogy* quality = lives in the prompt, never the tool's claim. These tags and the six delivery criteria are likely the *same axis seen twice* (tags = *what* it delivers; criteria = *how well*) — design them together. Display deferred.

**Still open (carried):** the six delivery criteria (the next stretch — the engine everything reconciles to; owner is already at its doorstep via the "quality" split); whether "the stance" is a fully named component; page structure & display (deferred by owner).

## Strategy (2026-07-23, earlier — CEWA benched; rubric ≠ ACME; phased comparison; stage-primary axis)

Reached in a discussion session sparked by the owner asking whether to borrow ideas from **arena.ai** (LMArena — the crowdsourced, blind, head-to-head LLM leaderboard that produces an Elo ranking). Five owner calls, most refining how the improvement model, the rubric, and the tool taxonomy relate.

- **OWNER CALL — CEWA is benched for the foreseeable future.** Stronger than the existing THREADS/current-state framing of "approval data hidden, pending authorisation to reinstate": the CEWA *angle* is off the table, not paused. Do not treat CEWA approval/badges as a live or near-term feature, and — importantly for strategy — **do not lean on "CEWA approval" as the site's authority/trust mechanism.** The authority to make authored judgements now comes from the owner-authored explanatory spine (ACME + stance + bias declaration), which is a stronger fit anyway: arena.ai's own credibility comes from a legible, transparent method, not an approvals body. A legible authored judgement beats both a crowd vote and an approvals badge — and it's the only one of the three the site actually still has.
- **OWNER CALL — the rubric is NOT ACME; it is a narrow rib hanging off it.** ACME is the broad, teacher-facing improvement spine; its subject is teaching-and-learning getting better, and AI is not its point. The **rubric** is a deliberately narrow slice underneath a stage, whose only question is *"for a given ACME stage, which AI tools help you do that stage better?"* The pedagogy is fully expressed upstream in ACME/the stage; the rubric inherits the stage as context and only judges how well a tool *delivers* against that need. This is the right parent for the existing "score delivery only; pedagogy lives in the prompt, never in the score" principle.
- **OWNER CALL — phased comparison; crowd-vote is out of scope.** In the low-content early phase, authored **rubric judgement is the only viable form**. Each authored judgement is itself a structured comparative sample, so the rubric is not just a stopgap — it *generates the dataset* later comparison forms read from (hence: keep phase-1 output *structured/consistent*, not freeform prose, or phase 2 starts from scratch). Once enough comparative samples accumulate, branch to **sample-derived** comparison forms ("tools ranked for this job", "this vs that", "best in class for X") — NOT arena-style crowdsourced Elo, which needs a voting user base a low-traffic educator site can't assume. *Still to rule: the rough trigger count to move phase 1 → phase 2 (agent floated ~15–20 tools scored on the same criteria).*
- **OWNER CALL — eventual data model is stage-primary, job-tagged (inverting today's use-category-primary axis).** Organise tools **primarily by ACME stage**, tag by job. Reason is branding/soul, not utility: organising by job is table stakes (every AI-tools directory does it) and can't be the brand; organising by stage is unique to this site and expresses the ACME spine structurally. This is the taxonomy-level expression of the existing doctrine "work-first entry, pedagogy-first soul" — so the resolution of the branding-vs-usability tension is: **stage is primary in structure/branding; job stays primary at the entry point** (a teacher arrives knowing "I need a rubric", not "I'm at the Evidence stage"). The job is the door, the stage is the house. Supersedes the primacy (not the existence) of DECISIONS → "Use-category is the primary tag axis".
- **NOT MUTUALLY EXCLUSIVE — job vs stage stay dual views.** Some jobs sit between stages; expect messy overlap. They co-exist as *presentations* freely, but for the underlying *data* only one is the stored tag and the other is derived — do not hand-maintain two independent stored taxonomies (the codebase's standing duplication-drift failure mode).
- **OWNER CALL — store `job` per tool; derive `stage` from a single `job → stage(s)` map.** Resolved 2026-07-23 after the owner worked through what "derived" means. The tool record carries its jobs (`useCategories`); stage is *not* typed onto tools — it's computed from a central mapping table, the same pattern `capabilities.json` already uses (store ids, derive labels — one source of truth, no drift). This is deliberately the **inverse** of "store stage" and it does **not** weaken the stage-primary branding call: stage stays primary in *presentation/structure* (the site leads and organises by stage); it's just *computed*, not hand-tagged. Two consequences the owner accepted: (a) **cross-stage falls out for free** — because the map is `job → stage(s)`, a job like `formative-checking` can point at both Method and Evidence, so a tool doing it spans both stages automatically, no per-tool work; the earlier "some jobs sit between stages" concern is answered by the data model itself rather than engineered around. (b) **Cross-stage-ness lives at the *job* level, not the individual tool** — every tool doing a given job inherits that job's stage(s); you cannot override one particular tool's stage without its jobs. The only case that would force *storing* stage is a tool whose correct stage genuinely isn't implied by its jobs (an editorial exception) — treated as a rare luxury not worth the second maintained field now. Supersedes the momentary "stored = stage" framing from the same session.

## Strategy (2026-07-22, later — the model is complete: ACME, dual-register)

The model is now fully settled: structure, stages, inputs, depth-meaning, and public register all closed. This entry is the canonical statement; the earlier 2026-07-22 entry below records how the stage *names* were chosen (superseded only on the E wording and the register).

- **OWNER CALL — the model carries two registers by design (progressive disclosure, the site's standard pattern):**
  - **The mnemonic — `ACME`** (Audience · Content · Method · Evidence). Punchy, memorable, holds in a teacher's head. Bonus the owner spotted: *acme* means the summit/peak — apt for an improvement model. Serves as the visual/label layer (loop graphic, nav).
  - **The working layer — a plain question per letter**, which is what a teacher actually thinks with and what teaches the model on first contact:

    | Letter | Label | Question |
    |---|---|---|
    | **A** | Audience | Who am I teaching? |
    | **C** | Content | What am I teaching? |
    | **M** | Method | How will I teach it so it sticks? |
    | **E** | Evidence | How is learning evident? |

  - Neither layer carries the load alone: the acronym is too abstract by itself, the questions too wordy to memorise. Together each covers the other's weakness.
- **The "Audience" brand clash is resolved by the two registers.** The owner's own copy line named *"pigeon hole your audience"* as a failure mode, so "Audience" describing students was a worry. Resolved: "Audience" is only the **mnemonic label**; the teacher-facing wording is the question "Who am I teaching?" — the loaded word does acronym duty, never describes the students in what a teacher reads.
- **OWNER CALL — agent = teacher, object = learner, throughout — but the stages are not forced into identical grammar.** A late owner challenge ("should this all be framed around the learner instead of the teacher?") was tested against the set and revealed the stages don't sit on one side: the **inner two (What, Method) are teacher-active** (the teacher selects content and designs method — method especially is the teacher's lever, and learner-framing it wrongly implies students choose the pedagogy), while the **outer two (Audience, Evidence) are learner-described** (who they are, what they learned). Forcing everything learner-side makes What go passive and How go wrong; forcing everything teacher-side makes Evidence self-centred. Conclusion: **the teacher is the agent of the loop (keeps the improvement engine and the Catalyst axis attached to a subject — a full learner-frame would orphan "who is improving?" and turn a *teacher improvement* model into a generic learning-design model, cutting against the site's "teacher holds the judgement" spine); the questions point at the learner as their object.** Grammar varies by stage honestly, because teaching *is* a teacher acting on behalf of learners.
- **`E` wording — "How is learning evident?" chosen** (over "What's the evidence of my teaching?", "What does the evidence say?", "What's the evidence of learning?"). The progression across the session: first fixed from *teaching* to *learning* (you can teach well and they still not learn — evidence of learning is the honest measure, and it feeds next turn's Audience better than evidence of teaching); then chosen in its "how is it evident" form because it asks the teacher to *define what learning even looks like* (success criteria, indicators) rather than just collect after the fact — the richest, most experienced-teacher-provoking phrasing, which is exactly the depth bar. **Known accepted cost:** it starts with "How", as does the Method question, so two questions open on "How"; judged worth it — provocation over tidiness — since the full questions aren't truly ambiguous.
- **Resolves the last open item (public-facing register): teachers see both — `ACME` labels as the anchor, the question as each letter's plain-English expansion.** With this, all four of the model's open items are closed. The teacher improvement model is complete; benchmark work is next.

## Strategy (2026-07-22 — the improvement model's stages: Who · What · How · Evidence)

- **OWNER CALL — the four stages are named `Who` · `What` · `How` · `Evidence`:**
  - **Who** — who am I teaching. Deliberately plural by default (a class, not a learner) — differentiation is not a separate stage or a closing check bolted on, it's what "who" already means once the honest answer is a range, not a single profile.
  - **What** — what am I teaching (curriculum fidelity, learning intentions).
  - **How** — how will I teach it so it sticks (delivery — CLT, Rosenshine, explicit instruction).
  - **Evidence** — did it work, for whom, what will I do about it. Closes the loop: this stage's output becomes the next turn's `Who`, matching the continuity ruling below almost word for word ("what the evidence says becomes the starting point of the next turn") — which is why the name was chosen over the alternatives it beat.
- **Differentiation folded into `Who`, not given its own stage.** It nearly became a closing "who isn't with me" check, separate from the front-loaded "who am I teaching." Collapsed on the reasoning that a single class is always multiple learners, so a real answer to "who am I teaching" already implies the range. `Evidence` inherits this for free — "did it work, for whom" asks the differentiation question without a dedicated slot.
- **`Evidence` chosen over `Check` / `Reflect` / `Feedback` / `Review`:**
  - `Review` — collides with shipped vocabulary (tool *reviews* are core benchmark terminology).
  - `Feedback` — already has a specific, opposite-direction job here: the Ring-3 scope decision below defines feedback as the *outward* half of marking ("marking gathers the evidence, feedback acts on it" — feedback *to students*). ## Strategy (2026-07-21, later — the improvement model's structure: stage · context · catalyst)

> Same caveat as the entry below: **the structure is committed, the contents are not.** The stages themselves remain a working sketch.

- **The model is continuous** (owner) — the final stage leads back into the first rather than terminating. What the evidence says becomes the starting point of the next turn. This also resolves the earlier "pre and post a learning event" requirement without a second set of questions: the post of one turn *is* the pre of the next.
- **Each stage carries two kinds of input, and they are inputs to the *improvement* process, not to the teaching process** (owner correction — an AI reading of them as inputs to the teaching question was wrong).
- **OWNER CALL — the three components are named `Stage` · `Context` · `Catalyst`:**
  - **Stage** — the step in the loop.
  - **Context** — situational input. Improves *this turn's* answer: better information, same you. (Prior knowledge, diagnostics, what was covered last year, known misconceptions, work samples, and data about your own practice — video, student voice, a colleague's eyes.)
  - **Catalyst** — developmental input. Improves *every future turn's* answer: better you, regardless of situation. (Coaching, mentoring, observation, moderation, reading research, developing subject or specialist knowledge.)
  - The distinction that keeps the two apart: **one changes this turn, the other changes every turn.** If the names stop carrying that, the columns blur.
  - Chosen over `fuel`, `spark`, `accelerant`, `lift`, `whetstone`, `impetus`, `traction`, `lever`, `kindling`, `nudge`, `provocation`. Deciding arguments: a catalyst causes change *without being consumed* (coaching and research aren't used up), it matches "context" in register, and it is the only shortlisted option that survives as a countable plural — stages will list several. Known imperfection: strictly a catalyst changes a reaction's *rate*, not its outcome, which undersells what coaching can do.
  - **Ruled out as labels because they collide with shipped vocabulary:** `prompt` (core benchmark vocabulary), `capability` (`capabilities.json`), `domain` (tools taxonomy, parent of work type), `practice` (an existing track id in `frontend/src/content/`).
- **Catalysts close the local-optimisation gap.** An AI objection held that a self-feeding loop only polishes a teacher's existing repertoire, with no route to a technique they have never met. Coaching, mentoring and observation are that route — new knowledge enters the loop through catalysts.
- **Catalysts, not geometry, carry the improvement claim.** A closed loop implies you return to where you started; a spiral implies rising. Since catalysts are what make the next turn higher, the model can stay drawn as a loop without the shape having to argue for improvement.
- **OWNER CALL — naming something in the model does not commit us to building content for it.** Behaviour management, mentoring and relationships can appear as catalysts or contexts while tool alignment and editorials for them stay deferred. Model completeness is cheap and honest; content is where the cost is. (This supersedes an AI "bloat" objection that conflated the two.)
